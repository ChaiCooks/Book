The Frog and the Nightingale

by Vikram Seth

üìú The Poem (Excerpt)

Every day the frog who'd sold her Songs for silver tried to scold her: "You must practice even longer Till your voice, like mine grows stronger. In the second song last night You got nervous in mid-flight. And, my dear, lay on more trills: Audiences enjoy such frills. You must make your public happier: Give them something sharper snappier. We must aim for better billings. You still owe me sixty shillings." Day by day the nightingale Grew more sorrowful and pale. Night on night her tired song Zipped and trilled and bounced along, Till the birds and beasts grew tired At a voice so uninspired And the ticket office gross Crashed, and she grew more morose - For her ears were now addicted To applause quite unrestricted, And to sing into the night All alone gave no delight. Now the frog puffed up with rage. "Brainless bird - you're on the stage - Use your wits and follow fashion. Puff your lungs out with your passion." Trembling, terrified to fail, Blind with tears, the nightingale Heard him out in silence, tried, Puffed up, burst a vein, and died. Said the frog: "I tried to teach her, But she was a stupid creature - Far too nervous, far too tense. Far too prone to influence. Well, poor bird - she should have known That your song must be your own. That's why I sing with panache: 'Koo-oh-ah! ko-ash! ko-ash!' And the foghorn of the frog Blared unrivalled through the bog.

üìñ Summary

In this modern fable, a frog with a "foghorn" voice exploits a talented nightingale who possesses a naturally beautiful voice. The frog becomes her "manager," charging admission for her performances and constantly criticizing her to assert control. Under his relentless pressure to practice longer, add more trills, and please the audience, the nightingale loses her natural gift and her health. When audiences tire of her mechanical performances and ticket sales crash, the frog berates her further. Trying to follow his final command to "puff your lungs out with your passion," the nightingale bursts a vein and dies. The frog blames her for being a "stupid creature" and continues his loud, tuneless croaking, unrivaled once again.

üéØ Key Themes

  • Exploitation and Manipulation The frog represents those who exploit talent for personal gain, using criticism and false expertise to control others.
  • The Destruction of Authenticity The nightingale loses her natural gift when she tries to conform to the frog's commercial demands and artificial standards.
  • Addiction to Validation The nightingale becomes addicted to "applause unrestricted," losing the ability to sing for pure joy.
  • Toxic Mentorship The poem critiques mentors who destroy their students' confidence while claiming to help them improve.

üîç Deep Dive Questions

1. Personification of Dark Human Nature

How does the personification of the frog's character represent the darker aspects of human nature? What human traits does he embody?

2. External Validation

How does the nightingale's fate serve as a cautionary tale about the dangers of external validation? What happens when we depend entirely on others' approval?

3. Language and Imagery

How does Vikram Seth's use of language and imagery contribute to the overall impact of the poem? Consider words like "foghorn," "addicted," and "morose."

4. Irony of the Ending

Discuss the irony in the frog's final statement: "your song must be your own." How is this hypocritical given his actions throughout the poem?
