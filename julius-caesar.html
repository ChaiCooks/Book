üìñ Scene Summary

The play's pivotal moment occurs in Act III, Scene I of Julius Caesar. Caesar's conceit and faith in his own invincibility are exposed when he arrives at the Capitol on the Ides of March and disregards the Soothsayer's and Artemidorus' warnings. Despite a fleeting concern about being discovered, the conspirators carry out their scheme by luring Antony away and kneeling before Caesar with a fictitious request for the recall of Publius Cimber. Caesar is immediately attacked after he refuses, declaring himself "constant as the Northern Star." Caesar cries in agony, "Et tu, Brute?" as Brutus delivers the final stroke. The deed is hailed by the conspirators as liberty. Later, Antony plans retaliation and predicts civil war while feigning devotion and being allowed to speak at Caesar's funeral.

üéØ Key Themes

  • Power and Ambition The tension between Caesar's ambition and the conspirators' fear of tyranny drives the tragic action.
  • Betrayal and Loyalty Brutus's betrayal of his friend Caesar in the name of Rome raises questions about conflicting loyalties.
  • Fate vs. Free Will Caesar ignores warnings (Soothsayer, Artemidorus's letter, Calpurnia's dream) yet seems fated to die.
  • Political Idealism vs. Reality Brutus's noble motives clash with the cynical political maneuvering of Cassius and Antony.
